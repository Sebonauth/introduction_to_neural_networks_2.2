Each biological neuron acts like a tiny information processor, receiving, integrating, and transmitting signals.

Continue

The dendrites are branching extensions that receive incoming signals from other neurons. They act like the receiving docks for incoming messages.

Continue

The signals are then transmitted to the cell body (or soma), where they are integrated. If the combined signal is strong enough, the neuron generates an electrical impulse called an action potential.

Build Your Vocab

Action Potential A rapid rise and fall in electrical membrane potential that transmits signals along the neuron's axon.

Continue

The axon is a long, slender projection that carries the action potential away from the cell body toward other neurons. It acts like a highway for the signal.

Continue

At the end of the axon are the synaptic terminals (synapses), where the neuron communicates with other neurons via chemical messengers called neurotransmitters. This process allows neurons to form complex networks that underlie all brain functions.

Continue

Why It Matters

Understanding the structure and function of biological neurons provides insights into how our brains process information and how we can model these processes in artificial systems.
